Description of interface

Three-dimensional (3D) printing is an additive manufacturing process that creates a physical object from a digital design. The process works by laying down thin layers of material in the form of liquid plastic and then fusing the layers together.


Users primarily interact with the control panel, where they can edit settings and launch the new print from the files on the SD card that they’ve inserted. It’s users’ responsibility to resupply the filament as needed by putting a new knob and connecting it with an extruder. The reset generally requires little to no supervision and is completed automatically.

General Observations:

Users spend hefty amounts of time pressing buttons in an attempt to start the printing process. Users spend extra time observing after finishing interactions with the control panel.

Users don’t really know what to do in case of the print error.


Questions

Empathy maps




Figure 1: Frustrated Jane is a liberal arts graduate student with a full-time job.

Her main problem with the interface is how non informative error messages are and the general high chance of failure of the printer. This Persona generalizes a knowledgeable user with prior experience of using the product, who will attempt to fix the issue by themself first. If it will not work they would be frustrated and probably would abandon the task altogether.



Figure 2: Confused John is a high school student attempting to print their first project that they’ve designed. Their main problem with the interface is how not newbie friendly it is. Even after orientation and some guided practice it’s difficult to fully understand what's going on. This Persona generalizes users who are very new to this type of technology and embark on their first solo project.
